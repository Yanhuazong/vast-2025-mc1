Questions

1. Design and develop visualizations and visual analytic tools that will allow Silas to explore and understand the profile of Sailor Shift's career.
a. Who has she been most influenced by over time?
b. Who has she collaborated with and directly or indirectly influenced?
c. How has she influenced collaborators of the broader Oceanus Folk community?

We designed and developed Melody Way, an interactive tool that let you explore how artists and genres connect and evolve, as shown in Figure 1. In its radial "galaxy," each artist's angle marks their latest release year and their distance shows influence, hover for quick stats. A surrounding timeline arc plots songs and albums by genre, with clicks revealing linked artists and labels below. Filters, search, and a built-in AI chatbot help you zoom to any role, genre, or influence path, while selecting an artist highlights collaborators and shows who inspired and was inspired by them.

  1. Figure 2 charts Sailor Shift's full career, while the close-up in Figure 3 isolates her incoming influence links (dashed lines). No single artist or work dominates those links: across 1983-2030, every influence appears only once. The sources span genres including Indie Folk, Dream Pop, Synthwave, and arrive via stylistic echoes (Sty), melodic interpolations (In), covers (Cov), lyrical references (Ly), and direct sampling (Sam). Taken together, the pattern shows Sailor drawing inspiration from a rotating cast of songs and albums, evolving through diversity rather than through allegiance to any one mentor or era.
  2. Figure 4 shows that Sailor Shift worked with dozens of collaborators including lyricists, composers, and performers such as Maya Jensen, Jonak Calloway, and Beatrice Albright. She released her music through labels like Coastal Harmony Studios, Vertical Ascent Records, and Oceanic Records. Although the schema defines influence links as work-to-work, every recorded influence from Sailor is artist-to-artist. As Figure 5 illustrates, she inspired groups such as Copper Canyon Ghosts and The Phantom Operators, as well as solo artists like Caisian Storm, underscoring the breadth and enduring impact of her creative reach.
  3. As shown in Figures 4 and 5, Sailor Shift, as both performer and lyricist, has influence radiating across the Oceanus Folk scene. She wrote lyrics for eight genre-defining albums, including Electric Reverie, Ballads for the Low Tide, and Artificial Sunset, work that peers later echoed. Her impact is most evident in Copper Canyon Ghosts, who directly sampled her vocals before releasing a string of notable Oceanus Folk recordings such as albums like Tide-Worn Tales and singles like Shadow in Smuggler's Cove, among others. Together, these links underscore Sailor's dual role as prolific contributor and catalyst for subsequent Oceanus Folk artists.

Using the filters in Melody Way, we can explore how the influence of Oceanus Folk has spread across the musical world.

  1. With Oceanus Folk selected as the influencing genre, we see all the artworks shaped by it, along with their contributing artists and associated record labels (Fig. 6). The line chart above the timeline reveals that Oceanus Folk's influence has not followed a smooth, continuous rise. Instead, it shows intermittent surges, with notable peaks in years like 2017 followed by quieter periods, such as the lull between 2017 and 2021.
  2. Figure 7 displays only the artworks from other genres that were influenced by Oceanus Folk, along with their contributors and associated record labels. Genres such as Indie Folk, Dream Pop, and Desert Rock show strong influence from Oceanus Folk. Since artists are positioned based on their overall influence and productivity with the most prominent artists appearing farther from the center, we can easily identify top artists, as marked by a white circle. Figure 8 shows detailed views of three specific artists selected from within this circle: Xiuying Yao (3 of 9 works), Min Fu (2 of 10), and Filippo Pelli (2 of 11), all of whom have multiple works influenced by Oceanus Folk.

Reflection Questions

1. This challenge pushed you to develop new techniques for visual representation?

Yes. The graph's scale led us to a three-layer design. We group nodes into artists, artworks, and labels and reveal links only on hover, eliminating hair-ball clutter. Artist nodes form a radial "galaxy": distance from the center encodes combined influence and productivity, angle marks the year of the latest release, and a slender tail extends backward to show how that influence has grown over time. Beneath the galaxy, a curved timeline pins each artwork to its release year and supports range filtering. The result lets users see who connects to whom, how strongly, and when without overload.

2. Did you participate in last year's challenge? If so, did your experience last year help prepare you for this year's challenge?

Some of our team members participated in last year's challenge. That experience helped us become more familiar with the overall process, including understanding the dataset structure, identifying storylines, and managing the timeline. It also gave us a clearer sense of what makes a compelling and informative visualization, which allowed us to plan and iterate more effectively this year.

3. What was the most difficult part of working on this year's data and what could have made it more accessible?

The most difficult part was the incompleteness of the dataset, especially for the years after 2030, where the number of artworks drops significantly. Additionally, many artwork-to-artwork influence links were missing for key artists like Sailor, making it difficult to trace her indirect influence. A more complete dataset with consistent records across all years and more comprehensive influence relationships would have made the analysis more accessible and accurate.
