Questions

 

1 Design and develop visualizations and visual analytic tools that will allow Silas to explore and understand the profile of Sailor Shifts career. 

a.        Who has she been most influenced by over time? 

b.       Who has she collaborated with and directly or indirectly influenced? 

c.        How has she influenced collaborators of the broader Oceanus Folk community? 

 

We designed and developed Melody Way, an interactive too that let you explore how artists and genres connect and evolve, as shown in Figure 1. In its radial galaxy, each artists angle marks their latest release year and their distance shows influence, hover for quick stats. A surrounding timeline arc plots songs and albums by genre, with clicks revealing linked artists and labels below. Filters, search, and a built‑in AI chatbot help you zoom to any role, genre, or influence path, while selecting an artist highlights collaborators and shows who inspired and was inspired by them.

a.      Figure 2 charts Sailor Shifts full career, while the close‑up in Figure 3 isolates her incoming influence links (dashed lines). No single artist or work dominates those links: across 1983‑2030, every influence appears only once. The sources span genres including Indie Folk, Dream Pop, Synthwave, and arrive via stylistic echoes (Sty), melodic interpolations (In), covers (Cov), lyrical references (Ly), and direct sampling (Sam). Taken together, the pattern shows Sailor drawing inspiration from a rotating cast of songs and albums, evolving through diversity rather than through allegiance to any one mentor or era.

 

b.     Figure 4 shows that Sailor Shift worked with dozens of collaborators including lyricists, composers, and performers such as Maya Jensen, Jonak Calloway, and Beatrice Albright. She released her music through labels like Coastal Harmony Studios, Vertical Ascent Records, and Oceanic Records. Although the schema defines influence links as work‑to‑work, every recorded influence from Sailor is artist‑to‑artist. As Figure 5 illustrates, she inspired groups such as Copper Canyon Ghosts and The Phantom Operators, as well as solo artists like Caisian Storm, underscoring the breadth and enduring impact of her creative reach.

 

c.      As shown in Figures 4 and 5, Sailor Shift as both performer and lyricist, her influence radiating across the Oceanus Folk scene. She wrote lyrics for eight genre‑defining albums, including Electric Reverie, Ballads for the Low Tide, and Artificial Sunset, work that peer later echoed. Her impact is most evident in Copper Canyon Ghosts, who directly sampled her vocals before releasing a string of notable Oceanus Folk recordings such as albums such as Tide‑Worn Tales and singles like Shadow in Smugglers Cove, among others. Together, these links underscore Sailors dual role as prolific contributor and catalyst for subsequent Oceanus Folk artists.

2 Develop visualizations that illustrate how the influence of Oceanus Folk has spread through the musical world. 

a.        Was this influence intermittent or did it have a gradual rise? 

b.       What genres and top artists have been most influenced by Oceanus Folk? 

c.        On the converse, how has Oceanus Folk changed with the rise of Sailor Shift? From which genres does it draw most of its contemporary inspiration? 

 

Using the filters in Melody Way, we can explore how the influence of Oceanus Folk has spread across the musical world.

a.      With Oceanus Folk selected as the influencing genre, we see all the artworks shaped by it, along with their contributing artists and associated record labels (Fig. 7). The line chart above the timeline reveals that Oceanus Folks influence has not followed a smooth, continuous rise. Instead, it shows intermittent surges, with notable peaks in years like 2017 followed by quieter periods, such as the lull between 2017 and 2021.

 

b.     Figure 8 displays only the artworks from other genres that were influenced by Oceanus Folk, along with their contributors and associated record labels. Genres such as Indie Folk, Dream Pop, and Desert Rock show strong influence from Oceanus Folk. Since artists are positioned based on their overall influence and productivity with the most prominent artists appearing farther from the center, we can easily identify top artists, as marked by a white circle. Figure 9 shows detailed views of three specific artists selected from within this circle: Xiuying Yao (3 of 9 works), Min Fu (2 of 10), and Filippo Pelli (2 of 11), all of whom have multiple works influenced by Oceanus Folk.

 

c.      The theme‑river in Figure 10 shows Oceanus Folk widening sharply after Sailor Shifts breakout in 2028; the thicker ribbon and larger circles inside it reflect a surge of new artists entering the genre. Figure 11 highlights how the influences on Oceanus Folk shifted over time: yearly influence pie charts turn from mostly yellow (self‑influence) to larger pink and green slices, indicating growing inputs from Rock sub‑genres such as Space Rock and Blues Rock, along with genres like Americana and Synthwave. Figure 12 quantifies this shift, showing the number of Oceanus Folk artworks influenced by the Folk genre and rock genre families over time for comparison.

3 Use your visualizations to develop a profile of what it means to be a rising star in the music industry. 

a.        Visualize the careers of three artists. Compare and contrast their rise in popularity and influence. 

b.      Using this characterization, give three predictions of who the next Oceanus Folk stars with be over the next five years.  

 

 

To define what it means to be a rising star in the music industry, we analyzed the careers of three artists with varying paths to recognition, using Melody Ways influence and collaboration data.

a.       We visualized the careers of Jay Walters, Kimberly Snyder, and Crimson Carriage to explore different rising patterns, as shown in Figure 13. Jay Walters has had a long, steady career since the 1980s, contributing as performer, lyricist, composer, and producer. His influential works like Wilderness Vows at Twilight and Unfinished Conversations show widespread impact. Kimberly Snyders shorter career includes consistent output and strong collaboration, with multiple works influencing others. Crimson Carriage is a new group with steady contributions, notable collaborations like Sailor Shift, and support from major labels. These cases reflect diverse success paths: long-term presence, rapid influence, and emerging potential, each tied to consistent output, collaboration, and creative roles.

b.       To predict the next Oceanus Folk stars, we looked for artists who (1) stay active post‑2035, (2) show expanding collaboration networks, (3) take on creative roles (e.g., performer), (4) work with established Oceanus‑focused labels, and (5) display rising influence, though the last metric might be under‑recorded because the possible gaps in the dataset. Using Melody Ways chatbot and graph checks, we identify The Brine Choir, Copper Canyon Ghost, and Maya Jensen as the strongest candidates. As shown in Figure 14, each is active in or after 2035, has collaborated with influential stars like Sailor Shift, released notable Oceanus‑Folk works, and maintains partnerships with major labels.

Reflection Questions (Optional)

 

1.     Given the task to develop visualizations for knowledge graphs, did you find that the challenge pushed you to develop new techniques for visual representation? 

Yes. The graphs scale led us to a three‑layer design. We group nodes into artists, artworks, and labels and reveal links only on hover, eliminating hair‑ball clutter. Artist nodes form a radial galaxy: distance from the center encodes combined influence and productivity, angle marks the year of the latest release, and a slender tail extends backward to show how that influence has grown over time. Beneath the galaxy, a curved timeline pins each artwork to its release year and supports range filtering. The result lets users see who connects to whom, how strongly, and when without overload.

 

2.     Did you participate in last years challenge? If so, did your experience last year help prepare you for this years challenge?

Some of our team members participated in last years challenge. That experience helped us become more familiar with the overall process, including understanding the dataset structure, identifying storylines, and managing the timeline. It also gave us a clearer sense of what makes a compelling and informative visualization, which allowed us to plan and iterate more effectively this year.

 

3.     What was the most difficult part of working on this years data and what could have made it more accessible?

The most difficult part was the incompleteness of the dataset, especially for the years after 2030, where the number of artworks drops significantly. Additionally, many artwork-to-artwork influence links were missing for key artists like Sailor, making it difficult to trace her indirect influence. A more complete dataset with consistent records across all years and more comprehensive influence relationships would have made the analysis more accessible and accurate.
